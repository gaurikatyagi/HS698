The Heart Rate Signal

Each beat is categorized by a QRS-complex. The R component is the upward surging large peak.

Moving Averages

Moving average is a useful notion as the amplitude of R peaks can change over time, it can also help eliminate parts of signal when sensors are moved. The secondary peak may also show an amplitude close to that of the R-peak. Thus a moving average concept can be used to iteratively change the moving average threshold and determine which R values seems to fit in to give the best results.

Analysis Methodology

The dataset comprises of timer readings and mv observations captured by an instrument. The approach mainly focusses on finding the positions of all the R-peaks in the dataset.

The R peaks can be found by determining each QRS complex region of interest and finding the local maxima in each of these regions. There are three broad ways of achieving this; first is to fit a curve on the region of interest (ROI) and find the peaks maxima. The second method entails determining the slope between each ROI and find the point where the slope reverses. The last method requires finding all points in the ROI and finding the position of the highest point.

As we can see from the heart beat plot on the left; apart from the R peak, the data also has P and T peaks. These might not be as sharp at the R peak, but to ensure that our algorithm does not detect these peaks as “R” values, we find the moving average of the signal and consider R points to only be those points which are above the moving average of the signal.

The frequency of measurement is found out using the Fourier transforms are also used. It takes a signal that repeats over time and determines the frequency of the signal. It uses the concept of transforming signals from time to frequency domain. As Wikipedia explains, “In the study of Fourier series, periodic functions are written as the sum of simple waves.”

Signal Filtering

Signals are mostly noisy. Noise can be reduced and R-peak estimation can be improved by leveraging Butterworth Filters. These filters act as a thresholding medium, allowing frequencies only within a range to maintain values. All other values are estimated to either the highest point or the lowest measure of the filter. However, this is not a hard threshold, that is signals are allowed to slightly fade into the threshold, allowing slightly higher/lower values, i.e. reducing its amplitude.

Note: Whether or not to filter a signal is a call the data scientist needs to make.

This methodology is important because a missed R value gives a R-R interval twice as big as the average R-R interval; an extra R value will halve the R-R value. Thus, increasing the R-R interval standard deviation.

Heart Rate

Once we have the frequency of Sampling and the R-peak values, averaging can be used to find the ‘beats per minute’ (BPM) measure.

Time Series Measures

  • BPM
  • IBI (inter-beat interval), or the R-R interval
  • SDNN, the standard deviation of intervals between heartbeats
  • SDSD, the standard deviation of successive differences between adjacent R-R intervals
  • RMSSD, the root mean square of successive differences between adjacent R-R intervals
  • pNN50/pNN20, the proportion of differences greater than 50ms / 20ms.

These “Heart Rate Variability” (HRV) measures, give information about how the heart rate varies over time.

Frequency Domain Measures

Frequency can be classified into HF (High Frequency), MF (Mid Frequency) and LF (Low Frequency) bands. The MF and HF bands are often taken together and just labeled ‘HF’. 0.04-0.15Hz is the LF band and 0.16-0.5Hz is the HF band. The LF band seems related to short-term blood pressure variation, the HF band to breathing rate.

HF and LF are found by re-sampling the signal in order to approximate the spectrum, then transform the re-sampled signal to the frequency domain.

Instead of transforming the BPM to the frequency measure, we are looking to transform the R-R intervals to the frequency domain. This is because, the heart rate speeds up and slows down, expressed as change in distance between R-R intervals.

However, Fourier Transform is based on evenly spaced data, (R-R intervals are not because of changing body needs). Thus, we create an evenly spaced timeline of R-R intervals (by interpolating the signal- resampling). The frequency is then found by calculating the spacing between the resampled R-R intervals.
